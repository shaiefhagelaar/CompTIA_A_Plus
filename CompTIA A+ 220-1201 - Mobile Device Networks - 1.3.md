The **CompTIA A+ 220-1201 (Core 1)** exam, specifically Objective 1.3, focuses on the connectivity and networking capabilities of mobile devices. As these devices are designed for portability, understanding how they transition between different wireless technologies is essential for troubleshooting and configuration.

---

## 1. Cellular Networks
Mobile devices rely on cellular providers to maintain data and voice connections over large geographic areas.

* **GSM vs. CDMA:** Older technologies. GSM (Global System for Mobile) used SIM cards, while CDMA (Code Division Multiple Access) often tied the handset to the carrier. In the modern 4G/5G era, these distinctions have mostly merged into LTE standards.
* **4G LTE (Long Term Evolution):** Supports speeds up to 100 Mbps for mobile users and 1 Gbps for stationary users.
* **5G (Fifth Generation):** Offers significantly higher speeds (up to 10–20 Gbps) and much lower latency. It operates across three frequency bands: low-band, mid-band, and high-band (mmWave).
* **Data Roaming:** Occurs when a device connects to a tower not owned by its home carrier.



---

## 2. Wireless Connectivity (Wi-Fi and PAN)
Beyond cellular, devices use short-range and local area wireless technologies to communicate.

* **Wi-Fi (802.11):** The primary way mobile devices access local networks and the internet. Modern devices support 802.11ac (Wi-Fi 5) and 802.11ax (Wi-Fi 6/6E).
* **Bluetooth (PAN):** Used for Personal Area Networks. It allows for "pairing" with peripherals like headphones, smartwatches, and car systems. 
    * *Process:* Enable Discovery > Select Device > Enter PIN/Confirm Passkey.
* **NFC (Near Field Communication):** A very short-range technology (centimeters). Its primary uses are:
    * **Contactless Payments:** Apple Pay, Google Pay.
    * **Identity/Bootstrap:** Quick pairing of Bluetooth devices or scanning "smart posters."

---

## 3. Positioning and Location Services
Mobile devices determine their physical location using a combination of "constellations" and local signals.

* **GPS (Global Positioning System):** A US-owned satellite-based radio navigation system. The device needs a line of sight to at least four satellites to calculate a 3D position (latitude, longitude, and altitude).
* **Assisted GPS (A-GPS):** Uses cellular towers and Wi-Fi signals to help the device find satellites faster, especially when indoors or in urban canyons.
* **Geofencing:** A software feature that uses GPS/Wi-Fi to trigger an action when a device enters or leaves a specific virtual boundary.



---

## 4. Connection Management & Security
Managing how a device connects is critical for both battery life and data security.

* **Airplane Mode:** A one-touch toggle that disables all wireless radios (Cellular, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, NFC, and GPS) to comply with airline regulations.
* **Hotspot:** Turns the mobile device into a Wi-Fi router, sharing its cellular data with other nearby devices.
* **Tethering:** Similar to a hotspot, but typically refers to a one-to-one connection via a USB cable or Bluetooth.
* **VPN (Virtual Private Network):** Establishes an encrypted "tunnel" over a public network (like hotel Wi-Fi) to ensure data privacy.

| **Cellular** | Kilometers | Long-range data and voice |
